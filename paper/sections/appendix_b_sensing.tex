% Appendix: sparse wall-pressure state estimation. Redone on the production v2
% (noBN) latents with the optimal target-conditioned (TCSI) sensor placement, and
% reporting absolute error in physical units, not R^2 alone. The central honest
% point is that latent recoverability (R^2) and physical estimation quality are
% different axes: the easiest-to-recover latent gives the worst lift estimate, and
% the lift is read most accurately straight off the pressure.

At deployment the vorticity field is not available; only the wall pressure is. We
ask how much of the latent state, the gust parameters, and the impact lift can be
recovered from a sparse set of $K$ wall-pressure taps over a pre-impact window,
and through which representation. The input is the span-averaged pressure on the
$192$ surface taps, and the pressure-to-latent map is a kernel-ridge regressor
with a radial-basis kernel on the flattened $K$-sensor by pre-impact-window
feature vector. Nonlinear estimators of this class (kernel ridge, a
temporal-convolutional network, a regularised perceptron, and a cross-validated
LSTM) perform comparably on the state; the recurrent model's advantage appears for
the scalar lift at short lead (Figure~\ref{fig:leadtime}). We use kernel ridge
throughout the cross-family comparison for a uniform, low-variance estimator
across families. This is a feasibility study, not a closed controller, and we report
where it works and where it does not.

\paragraph{Optimal placement clusters at the leading edge and matters most at very
low $K$.} We place the $K$ sensors by a target-conditioned greedy selection
(TCSI), conditioned on recovering the predictive latent, and compare it against
QR-pivoting (qDEIM) \cite{manohar2018sensors}, uniform, and random placement. The
selected taps cluster on both surfaces near the leading edge, where the gust
impinges (Figure~\ref{fig:sensor_placement}a). At $K = 2$ the placement is
decisive: TCSI recovers the predictive state at $R^2 = 0.80$ against $0.44$ for
qDEIM and $0.52$ for uniform. By $K \ge 4$, however, the wall pressure is
information-rich enough that the selection method barely separates
($R^2 \approx 0.86$ to $0.89$ for all three; Figure~\ref{fig:sensor_placement}b).
Optimal placement buys the most when sensors are scarcest. The robustness of the
greedy, target-conditioned selection over a marginal-attribution ranking (such as
SHAP or permutation importance) is expected here: the surface pressures are
strongly collinear, so a method that scores sensors individually double-counts
the shared information, whereas the greedy selection conditions each pick on those
already chosen and spreads the array. The gap is largest at the smallest $K$,
where redundancy is most costly.

\paragraph{The predictive state is the most pressure-recoverable.} With the TCSI
array and a kernel-ridge estimator the predictive latent is markedly the most
recoverable across encoder families (Figure~\ref{fig:observability}a): at $K = 8$
the held-out test\_b state $R^2$ is $0.89$ at $d = 64$ and $0.87$ at $d = 32$,
against $0.58$ to $0.67$ for the reconstructive autoencoder and $0.32$ to $0.49$
for POD. The contrast is sharpest at matched capacity: at $d = 64$ the predictive
latent recovers at $R^2 = 0.89$ against $0.58$ for the reconstructive autoencoder
and $0.32$ for POD, and at $d = 32$ at $0.87$ against $0.63$ and $0.42$. The
energy-optimal POD coordinates are the hardest to read from pressure,
the deployment counterpart of the representation result in the main text. Decoding
the pressure-estimated latent makes the recovery physical
(Figure~\ref{fig:flow_recovery}): from eight taps the leading-edge vortex and
shear layer are recovered close to the oracle decode, and even two taps capture
the gross impingement.

\paragraph{Recoverability is not estimation quality, and $C_L$ is read directly.}
A high recovery $R^2$ does not imply an accurate physical estimate, and the
pressure study makes the distinction concrete. The three-dimensional
reconstructive latent is the \emph{easiest} to recover from pressure (state
$R^2 = 0.91$ at $K = 2$, the highest of any family) yet routing the impact-lift
estimate through it gives the \emph{worst} $C_L$: a mean absolute error of $1.0$
against a $C_L$ spread of $1.37$, while the predictive latent gives $0.49$
(Figure~\ref{fig:observability}b). The lift itself is read most accurately
straight off the pressure: a direct pressure-to-$C_L$ regressor reaches a mean
absolute error of $0.39$ at the impact frame, better than any latent-mediated
route, because the imminent lift is already imprinted on the wall. The deployment
value of the predictive latent is therefore the recoverable \emph{forecastable
state} it hands to the predictor, not the instantaneous lift, which needs no
latent. We report the physical estimate in $C_L$ units rather than the state $R^2$
alone precisely because the two measure different things.

\paragraph{Parameters and the out-of-distribution boundary.} The gust parameters
recover directly from pressure on test\_b, with the core diameter best and the
wall-normal offset weakest ($(G, D, Y)$ $R^2 = (0.73, 0.89, 0.65)$ at $K = 8$), in
keeping with $Y$'s weak resolution throughout the paper. On the
out-of-distribution test\_c set ($|G| = 4$) every estimator and every family gives
negative state $R^2$ (between $-0.3$ and $-0.9$) and a large lift error, the same
three-dimensional observability boundary of \S\ref{sec:disc_limits} that bounds
the rest of the model.

\paragraph{Predicting the impact before it occurs.} The deployment target is the
impact itself, ahead of time. Ending the pre-impact pressure window $\tau$ frames
before impact and predicting the impact-frame state and lift
(Figure~\ref{fig:leadtime}), the impact state stays recoverable to eight instants
ahead with a gentle degradation, and kernel ridge and the LSTM are comparable
across the lead (state $R^2$ between $0.80$ and $0.83$). The impact lift is read
accurately to about six instants ahead before degrading. Both estimators are
selected by five-fold cross-validation on the training set, and the selection is
not overfit: the cross-validated state $R^2$ ($0.83$) matches the held-out value
($0.80$), and the search prefers a small recurrent model. The LSTM reads the
scalar lift more accurately at every lead (mean absolute error $0.25$ at the
impact frame and $0.39$ at a six-frame lead, against the kernel ridge's $0.30$ and
$0.45$; lift $R^2 = 0.89$ against $0.77$ at the impact frame), an advantage that
survives cross-validated selection and reflects its use of the temporal signature
of the approaching gust. The imminent impact is therefore predictable a handful of
instants before it occurs, the regime a controller would act in, and degrades into
the rollout-limited regime of the closed-loop pilot beyond that lead.

\paragraph{The closed loop is rollout-limited, not estimator-limited.} Chaining
the pressure estimator, the predictor rollout, and the observable probe into a
closed loop does not yet meet a tight control tolerance: at $H = 16$ only $18\%$
of test\_b encounters fall within $10\%$ of the true $C_L$, against a target of
$80\%$. The informative point is that this gate fails even for an oracle that is
handed the true impact latent and the true parameters ($18\%$ as well), so the
tolerance is bounded by the irreducible error of the predictor and probe, not by
the pressure estimator. The honest reading is that the representation and the
pressure observability are in place in distribution, and the closed loop is
limited by the rollout horizon rather than by the estimator; more frequent
re-estimation from pressure during the rollout, or a longer scheduled-sampling
horizon, is the route to a usable controller. We report this as a pathway, not a
result.
